Uncertainty 1: choice of baseline windows

Different baselines could change the magnitude of shifts and the ranking of seasons (whether spring remains the largest)

Client focuses on ‘how large is the shift?’ and ‘which season is changing most?’, if the magnitudes move meaningfully under an alternative baseline, the conclusion could change.

Maybe use a more recent window, or a warmer historical baseline (1971-2000) will tend to reduce the estimated shift.

The recommendation that there is a sustained warming shift is unlikely to change unless alternative window choices reduce multiple season-metric shifts to near zero, however the claim that ‘spring shows the largest shift’ could be downgraded if alternative baselines consistently change the top-ranked season or compress differences between seasons.

Uncertainty 2: Min/max and distributional conclusions may be influenced by a small number of extreme years

Seasonal max/min and distribution tails are sensitive to a small number of unusually hot/cold years. This matters because the current conclusion highlights spring maximum as the largest shift, that result could be partly driven by a few extreme years in the recent period.

Extremely warm years toward the end of the record can inflate recent-period maxima and make ‘extremes intensifying’ look stronger. If removing the most extreme 1 or 2 years could change the estimated shift/trend materially, then extremes-related claims reduce magnitude or change the estimated shift/trend materially, then extremes-related stable after excluding extremes, then the statement that spring maximum shows the largest shift is more defensible.

Uncertainty 3: Long-run homogeneity and non-climatic influences

Changes in observing practices (e.g. station/network characteristics, instrumentation, local environment) could induce non-climatic influence that affect the precise magnitude of long-run change. However, this issue is more likely affect the exact size of estimated shirts than to reverse the direction of change.
